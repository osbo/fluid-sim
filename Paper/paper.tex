\begin{abstract}
Real-time physics pipelines are bottlenecked by sparse linear solves.
The required preconditioner must capture long-range coupling while remaining cheap enough
to apply once per frame on a GPU.
Existing learned preconditioners follow the nonzero pattern of the system matrix, so
information between distant unknowns has to crawl through many message-passing layers; the
classical alternatives that do see globally (incomplete Cholesky, algebraic multigrid)
serialize on triangular solves or V-cycle barriers and miss the per-frame budget.
We learn a preconditioner on a hierarchical block partition computed once from the index
geometry: a transformer keeps dense diagonal blocks at full resolution, and a second
transformer summarizes every off-diagonal block at a single small token count regardless of
how big the block is, so total work stays linear in $N$. A cosine-similarity Hutchinson loss
trains the network without ever asking it to match $A^{-1}$ pointwise --- it only has to
align the preconditioned image of a probe with the probe itself, leaving the network free to
park the spectrum of $MA$ wherever convergence is easiest. The PCG iteration runs as a
single CUDA Graph of batched GEMMs over contiguous tensors, with no triangular solves and
no data-dependent branching.
On a stiff multiphase pressure-Poisson benchmark with up to $100\!:\!1$ density contrast,
the resulting solver stays above $22$\,fps from $N\!=\!1{,}024$ through $N\!=\!16{,}384$
while every classical and learned baseline we evaluate drops below $20$\,fps before the
top of that range. At $N\!=\!8{,}192$ we measure a $2.1\times$ speedup over GPU Jacobi
(the fastest classical GPU baseline), $24\times$ over AMGX \textsc{multicolor\_dilu}, and
$3.3\times$ over neural SPAI~\cite{Yang2025sparse} re-trained per scale on the same
benchmark with its CUDA apply path.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ===========================================================================

Real-time simulation of fluids, soft bodies, and coupled multiphysics systems often reduces,
at every time step, to solving a large sparse linear system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$.
In many pipelines this system is symmetric positive-definite (SPD), such as pressure or
stress solves attacked with preconditioned conjugate gradient (PCG)~\cite{saad2003iterative}.
Many
simulation pipelines bypass this entirely (position-based dynamics, explicit SPH) or use
nonsymmetric Krylov variants and direct solvers, but for the SPD systems that dominate
incompressible-fluid and elastic-solid pipelines, PCG remains the workhorse. The
preconditioner $M\!\approx\!A^{-1}$ is the dominant design variable: it must be cheap to
apply and must cluster the eigenvalues of $MA$ tightly enough to drive PCG to its tolerance
in a few dozen iterations. Classical preconditioners --- Jacobi, incomplete Cholesky/LU
(IC/ILU)~\cite{lin1999incomplete}, sparse approximate inverses (SPAI)
\cite{kolotilina1993factorized,benzi1996sparse}, and algebraic multigrid
(AMG)~\cite{ruge1987algebraic} --- are well-understood and excel in many regimes,
particularly when the matrix is reused across many solves so a heavy setup amortizes. In an
interactive setting, however, $A$ changes at every frame, so any preconditioner whose setup
cost approaches the per-frame budget cannot amortize; this affects IC and AMG most because
their setup is sequential and irregular~\cite{naumov2015amgx}, and IC application is further
bottlenecked on triangular solves whose data hazards resist GPU
parallelization~\cite{liu2016synchronization,yamazaki2020performance}.
Figure~\ref{fig:teaser} summarizes this narrative early: partition prior (a), learned
preconditioner structure (b), and resulting multiscale residual-correction behavior (c),
with panel~(d) included as an application-context illustration of the target multiphase
Poisson setting rather than as evidence of full frame-to-frame simulator integration.

Machine learning offers an appealing alternative: given a distribution of simulation states,
a neural preconditioner can be trained once and amortized across frames at near-constant,
predictable inference cost. Graph neural networks (GNNs) have become the dominant
architecture for this purpose~\cite{Li2023learning,hausner2023neural,chen2024gnp,Yang2025sparse},
because the natural graph interpretation of a sparse matrix lets message-passing layers
propagate information through its nonzero pattern. However, this same choice imposes two
fundamental limitations that become acute in real-time simulation. First, GNN preconditioners
typically inherit the sparsity pattern of $A$ or of an incomplete factor of $A$: non-adjacent
interactions are either ignored entirely or only approximated through repeated graph
convolutions, which require many layers to propagate long-range information and tend to
oversmooth local detail along the way~\cite{chen2020simple,trifonov2025gnn}. Second, the
dominant operations at apply time---triangular solves on a learned IC factor, or sparse
matrix-vector products on a learned SPAI---are bandwidth-bound and either serialize on data
hazards~\cite{liu2016synchronization} or scatter through irregular memory access.

\paragraph{From PCG geometry to hierarchical structure.}
PCG's convergence rate is governed by the spectrum of $MA$, so the ideal preconditioner is
$M\!=\!A^{-1}$; any practical alternative trades approximation quality for cheapness of
representation and application. Although $A$ is sparse, $A^{-1}$ is generally dense, so the
relevant question is what \emph{structure} makes $A^{-1}$ cheap to store and apply
approximately. That structure is determined by the index ordering of $A$. In modern
simulation codes, degrees of freedom are commonly already arranged along a space-filling
curve (Morton/Z, Hilbert) or a bandwidth-reducing permutation (reverse Cuthill--McKee), not
because the linear solver wants it but because the surrounding pipeline does: spatial-hash
and BVH data structures used for neighbor search, broad-phase collision, and parallel
particle access build their indices from exactly these
orderings~\cite{karras2012maximizing,teschner2003optimized,ihmsen2011parallel}. Under such
orderings, the nonzeros of $A$ cluster near the main diagonal: near-diagonal entries describe
spatially local interactions, while far-diagonal entries, when they exist, describe distant
ones.

The corresponding inverse $A^{-1}$ is dense, but it inherits a softer form of this geometry:
sub-blocks of $A^{-1}$ associated with index ranges that are well-separated along the
ordering have rapidly decaying singular values, with the numerical rank shrinking as the
separation grows. \emph{Hierarchical matrices}, or
$\mathcal{H}$-matrices~\cite{hackbusch1999sparse,hackbusch2002adaptive,borm2003introduction},
are the data structure that exploits this prior: they represent $A$ or $A^{-1}$ as a
recursive partition into dense diagonal blocks plus low-rank off-diagonal blocks, with the
admissible rank shrinking as off-diagonal tiles move further from the main diagonal. The
fast multipole method (FMM)~\cite{greengard1987fast} exploits the same prior more
aggressively for translation-invariant kernels, factoring far-field interactions through
coarse low-dimensional surrogates; an $\mathcal{H}$-matrix preconditioner is, architecturally,
an FMM-shaped operator on the assembled system. Multigrid analysis~\cite{briggs2000multigrid}
relies on a related hierarchy, but interleaves smoothing with restriction/prolongation across
levels. Whereas multigrid alternates across levels, FMM and $\mathcal{H}$-matrix application
use a flat one-pass structure (coarse summary, far-field broadcast, local correction) that we
adopt below.

\paragraph{Approach.}\label{intro:hierarchical}\hypertarget{intro:hierarchical}{}%
We anchor the preconditioner to a weak-admissibility $\mathcal{H}$-matrix
partition~\cite{hackbusch2002adaptive}, computed analytically from the leaf-index geometry
and reused for every frame. With minimum block size $L$ and $K = N/L$ leaves, the partition
consists of $K$ dense diagonal blocks of size $L\!\times\!L$ (one per leaf) and a set of
off-diagonal tiles, each spanning $S\!\times\!S$ leaves with $S\!=\!2^{j}$, indexed by leaf
coordinates. A two-stream transformer operates on this layout: a
\emph{diagonal} stream emits one factor matrix per leaf at full rank, and an
\emph{off-diagonal} stream emits one factor matrix per tile at a constant coarse token count
$L_s\!=\!L/p$, regardless of $S$. The implied full-resolution block then has rank at most
$L_s$ on a $SL\!\times\!SL$ region, so the rank fraction $L_s/(SL)$ shrinks automatically as
$S$ grows---exactly the structural bias $\mathcal{H}$-matrix compression already calls for.
To break the locality of within-block attention without giving up the $\Theta(N)$ budget, we
route information between layers through \emph{highway} buffers organized as axial
(row/column-band) buffers and a single global summary token
(Sec.~\ref{sec:method:highways}).
Teaser panel~(c) visualizes the resulting multiscale residual-correction behavior, where a
single application propagates information beyond nearest-neighbor stencil support.

\paragraph{A loss with no preferred eigenvalue location.}\label{intro:loss}\hypertarget{intro:loss}{}%
We train this network self-supervised with a cosine-similarity Hutchinson probe loss on the
preconditioned image $MA\mathbf{z}$ of random probes $\mathbf{z}$. The conceptual move,
relative to the Frobenius- and SAI-style objectives used by prior learned
preconditioners~\cite{Yang2025sparse}, is to replace a \emph{distance between vectors}
($\|\tfrac{1}{\|A\|}AM\mathbf{z}-\mathbf{z}\|_2$, which compares two specific points in
$\mathbb{R}^N$) with a \emph{distance between subspaces} (the angle between the lines spanned
by $\mathbf{z}$ and $MA\mathbf{z}$ in $\mathbb{R}^N$). The cosine objective is therefore
invariant under any positive rescaling of $M$, which exactly matches PCG: PCG cares only
about the direction $MA$ sends each probe, not the magnitude. Concretely, the loss imposes
no preferred location or scale for the eigenvalues of $MA$ --- it only asks that the
preconditioned probe align angularly with its source --- so the network is free to cluster
the spectrum wherever angular alignment is easiest. We formalize this as
Prop.~\ref{prop:cos-subspace} (\S\ref{sec:training:loss}) and verify it directly on the
spectrum of $MA$ in Fig.~\ref{fig:loss:eigenvalues}. This matters for stiff multiphase
systems whose spectra do not naturally cluster around $1$.

\paragraph{One CUDA Graph for the whole iteration.}\label{intro:cudagraph}\hypertarget{intro:cudagraph}{}%
Every preceding choice --- analytically determined partition, fixed coarse rank, no
triangular solves, allocation-free apply --- cooperates to remove the data dependencies that
would normally fragment a CG iteration into separately dispatched kernels. The full PCG
inner loop --- preconditioner apply, sparse matvec, residual update, scalar reductions ---
becomes a single fixed sequence of batched GEMMs and reductions over preallocated contiguous
tensors with no data-dependent branching. We capture it end-to-end as a single CUDA Graph
and replay it once per iteration, eliminating CPU dispatch overhead between every kernel of
every iteration. In our measurements, this is a major source of the speedup we
report against classical GPU preconditioners.

\paragraph{Contributions.} In short, we contribute:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}\setlength{\parsep}{0pt}
  \item \textbf{[\textsc{c1}]} A neural preconditioner that bakes the $\mathcal{H}$-matrix
    block partition in as a structural prior, with a fixed coarse rank on every off-diagonal
    tile so total work stays $\Theta(N)$
    (see \hyperref[intro:hierarchical]{intro paragraph \textsc{c1}}, full method in
    \S\ref{sec:method:diag}--\ref{sec:method:offdiag}).
  \item \textbf{[\textsc{c2}]} Highway buffers that let the network move information
    \emph{between} blocks without growing the budget, fixing the locality limitation that
    pure block-attention would otherwise have
    (see \hyperref[intro:hierarchical]{intro paragraph \textsc{c1}}, details in
    \S\ref{sec:method:highways}).
  \item \textbf{[\textsc{c3}]} A cosine-similarity Hutchinson training loss that minimizes a
    distance between \emph{subspaces} --- the angle between the lines spanned by $\mathbf{z}$
    and $MA\mathbf{z}$ in $\mathbb{R}^N$ --- rather than the distance between \emph{vectors}
    that Frobenius- and SAI-style objectives use. This exactly matches PCG's scale-invariance
    and frees the network to choose where in the spectrum to cluster $MA$ (formalized in
    Prop.~\ref{prop:cos-subspace}; see \hyperref[intro:loss]{intro paragraph \textsc{c3}},
    full derivation and ablation in \S\ref{sec:training:loss} and
    \S\ref{sec:results:training}).
  \item \textbf{[\textsc{c4}]} An allocation-free apply path with no triangular solves, no
    data-dependent branching, and no per-kernel CPU dispatch --- the entire PCG inner loop
    captured as a single CUDA Graph
    (see \hyperref[intro:cudagraph]{intro paragraph \textsc{c4}}, evaluation in
    \S\ref{sec:application}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ===========================================================================

\paragraph{Classical iterative solvers.}
Preconditioning sparse SPD systems is a textbook concern of scientific
computing~\cite{saad2003iterative}. Canonical algebraic preconditioners include Jacobi,
incomplete Cholesky/LU (IC/ILU), sparse approximate inverses (SPAI), and algebraic
multigrid (AMG)~\cite{lin1999incomplete,kolotilina1993factorized,benzi1996sparse,ruge1987algebraic}.
These methods are mature, well analyzed, and remain
default choices in scientific computing. IC and ILU mirror the elimination structure of $A$
within a chosen sparsity pattern, giving strong spectral approximation per unit memory.
SPAI methods approximate $A^{-1}$ directly as a sparse matrix, exposing parallelism by
replacing triangular solves with a sparse matvec at apply time, with the choice of sparsity
pattern controlling the approximation/cost trade-off. AMG constructs a hierarchy of
coarsened operators algebraically and achieves near-optimal convergence on elliptic
problems~\cite{ruge1987algebraic,naumov2015amgx}. These methods are at their best when the
system is reused across many solves so a careful setup amortizes. In contrast, the regime we
target --- $A$ changing every frame at interactive rates --- lies outside their most favorable
operating point, which motivates the amortized neural alternative pursued in this paper. The known GPU costs
of triangular solves~\cite{liu2016synchronization,yamazaki2020performance} and of AMG
setup/V-cycle communication~\cite{naumov2015amgx} bear directly on this regime rather than
on the methods themselves.

\paragraph{Neural operators and learned PDE solvers.}
Neural operators including Fourier-based~\cite{li2021fourier},
DeepONet-based~\cite{lu2021learning}, and graph-based~\cite{li2020neural,li2020multipole}
variants learn function-space mappings between coefficient and solution fields and
generalize across coefficient distributions, and have been deployed both as PDE surrogates
and as inner preconditioners for flexible Krylov solvers~\cite{rudikov2024neural}.
Physics-informed networks~\cite{raissi2019physics} embed PDE residuals directly into the
loss to enforce equation consistency without labeled solutions. Together these works
establish that neural networks can capture the global solution behavior of an operator
family from data. In contrast, the present paper shares the same broader ambition but
operates one rung lower in the stack, on the assembled
algebraic system rather than the underlying PDE.

\paragraph{Learning-based preconditioners.}
A productive line of work trains GNNs to produce a learned incomplete factorization of $A$:
the network emits the nonzero values of a triangular factor on a chosen sparsity pattern,
replacing the heuristic fill-in and ordering choices of classical IC with a forward pass
trained on a distribution of matrices~\cite{Li2023learning,hausner2023neural,trifonov2024linear}.
Yang \emph{et al.}~\cite{Yang2025sparse} develop a complementary direction in which a GNN
emits a sparse approximate inverse in factored $GG^\top$ form, evaluated as two SpMVs at
apply time, and show that a scale-invariant Frobenius (``SAI'') loss enables effective
self-supervised training without ever forming a solution vector --- an idea our own
self-supervised objective builds on. Chen~\cite{chen2024gnp} reports strong performance
across the SuiteSparse collection on ill-conditioned problems where classical IC and AMG
struggle, demonstrating the breadth of the GNN preconditioner approach.
Trifonov~\emph{et al.}~\cite{trifonov2025gnn} argue the converse from the architectural
side: message-passing GNNs --- and even graph-transformer variants that route through
purely local neighborhoods --- are fundamentally unable to approximate the non-local
elimination structure of sparse triangular factors, motivating architectures (like
ours) that carry global information through explicit channels rather than relying on
deeper local message passing. The present paper is in this lineage, and our point of departure is structural ---
described in the next section --- rather than a critique of the algorithmic choices these
works make.

\paragraph{Hierarchical neural architectures for PDEs.}
A parallel line of work embeds FMM- or $\mathcal{H}$-matrix-style hierarchical structure
directly into neural network architectures. $\mathcal{H}$-matrix-shaped multiscale
networks~\cite{Fan2019multiscale}, multipole graph neural operators~\cite{li2020multipole},
neural FMM/Green's-function operators~\cite{Fognini2025neural}, and neural-HSS
solvers~\cite{Sittoni2026neural} each demonstrate that hierarchical inductive biases let a
network represent long-range couplings without relying on fully connected attention or deep
stacks of purely local message passing. In a setting closer to ours, neural approximate
inverse preconditioners~\cite{Xu2025neural} and multigrid-inspired neural iterative
solvers~\cite{Lyu2026multigrid,Luz2020learning} use related structure to accelerate an
outer iteration. Collectively, this literature makes a strong case that an explicit
hierarchical/coarsening prior is a productive way to scale neural PDE machinery, and our
work adopts the same prior.

\paragraph{Hierarchical matrix techniques.}
Classical $\mathcal{H}$- and $\mathcal{H}^2$-matrix
arithmetic~\cite{hackbusch1999sparse,hackbusch2002adaptive,borm2003introduction} gives a
near-linear-cost framework for storing and applying operators whose well-separated
sub-blocks are low-rank, with mature machinery for adaptive partitioning, low-rank
truncation, and approximate factorization. HODLR variants~\cite{Hartland2023hodlr} apply the
same structural prior to dense Hessians in PDE-constrained inverse problems. The
partitioning machinery developed in this body of work is what we instantiate. The
contribution of the present paper lies in how the off-diagonal factors are produced and
applied, which is the subject of Sec.~\ref{sec:method}.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% ===========================================================================

\paragraph{Conjugate gradient and the role of the spectrum.}
CG's convergence is dictated by how the eigenvalues of $A$ are arranged on the real line.
Concretely, the error after $k$ iterations satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \|\mathbf{e}_k\|_A
  \;\le\; \|\mathbf{e}_0\|_A\,\min_{p\in\mathcal{P}_k,\,p(0)=1}\;
          \max_{\lambda\in\sigma(A)}|p(\lambda)|,
  \label{eq:cg-poly}
\end{equation}
so CG only ``sees'' $A$ through how small a polynomial that is $1$ at the origin can be made
on $\sigma(A)$. Two consequences of \eqref{eq:cg-poly} drive every preconditioner choice in
this paper. \emph{First}, the textbook bound in terms of the condition number
$\kappa\!=\!\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ is loose: it assumes the eigenvalues fill the
interval $[\lambda_{\min},\lambda_{\max}]$, whereas in practice the \emph{distribution}
matters more than the ratio. \emph{Second}, a degree-$k$ polynomial can vanish on $k$
clusters at once, so if $\sigma(A)$ collapses to $c$ tight clusters CG converges in
$\sim\!c$ iterations regardless of where the clusters live on the line. A few isolated
outlier eigenvalues only cost one extra iteration each. The preconditioning objective is
therefore not ``make $MA$ close to $I$,'' but ``make $\sigma(MA)$ a handful of tight clusters
away from zero'' --- the absolute location of those clusters is essentially free. This
objective is classical preconditioning theory, not a novelty claim of this paper; our
novelty is the fixed-structure neural realization that achieves it under strict real-time
GPU constraints.

\paragraph{Hierarchical matrices.}
An $\mathcal{H}$-matrix~\cite{hackbusch1999sparse,hackbusch2002adaptive,borm2003introduction}
represents a dense matrix as a recursive block partition in which diagonal blocks stay
dense, off-diagonal blocks are stored as low-rank factors $UV^\top$, and the admissible rank
decreases as the blocks sit further off the diagonal. Two flavors of admissibility appear
in the literature: \emph{strong admissibility}~\cite{hackbusch1999sparse} produces many
small geometrically separated blocks, and \emph{weak admissibility}~\cite{hackbusch2002adaptive}
produces fewer, larger ones --- a trade we lean on because larger uniform blocks are exactly
what a GPU likes to batch (see also HODLR~\cite{Hartland2023hodlr}). The structural
justification for low-rank off-diagonal blocks is that the underlying Green's function is
smooth away from the diagonal, so an off-diagonal block needs only $O(\log^d\varepsilon^{-1})$
columns to capture the interaction to accuracy $\varepsilon$ --- a rank that grows with
precision, not block size. Classical $\mathcal{H}$-matrix arithmetic runs in $O(N\log N)$
to $O(N\log^2 N)$.

\paragraph{Hutchinson trace estimation and probe spectra.}
Any random probe $\mathbf{z}\in\mathbb{R}^N$ with $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{z}\mathbf{z}^\top]\!=\!I$
satisfies the Hutchinson identity
$\mathbb{E}_\mathbf{z}[\mathbf{z}^\top B\mathbf{z}]\!=\!\operatorname{tr}(B)$~\cite{hutchinson1989stochastic},
which is the conventional reason for using such probes. The reason we use them is different.
Decompose $\mathbf{z}$ in the eigenbasis of $B$ as $\mathbf{z}\!=\!\sum_i\alpha_i\mathbf{u}_i$;
isotropy gives $\mathbb{E}[\alpha_i^2]\!=\!1$ for every $i$. That is, an isotropic probe
puts the same expected energy on every eigenmode of $B$. Any scalar built from
$(\mathbf{z},B\mathbf{z})$ therefore measures $B$'s action across the entire spectrum
uniformly, with no bias toward smooth or oscillatory modes. This \emph{spectral whiteness}
is the property that makes the cosine probe loss of
\hyperref[intro:loss]{paragraph \textsc{c3}} a faithful global indicator of preconditioner
quality.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
% ===========================================================================

\paragraph{Notation.}
Throughout this section, $A\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$ is the assembled sparse SPD system,
with degrees of freedom indexed along a space-filling-curve or bandwidth-reducing ordering.
The $N$ indices are partitioned into $K=N/L$ contiguous \emph{leaves} of size $L$; this
induces an $\mathcal{H}$-matrix partition consisting of $K$ diagonal leaf blocks and
$M_\mathcal{H}$ unique weakly-admissible \emph{off-diagonal tiles}, with tile $m$ spanning
$S_m\!\times\!S_m$ leaves (so covering an $S_mL\!\times\!S_mL$ region of $A$). The network
operates at channel width $d$ and uses $n_l$ transformer layers per attention stack. The
diagonal stream operates at full per-node resolution $L$ per leaf; the off-diagonal stream
operates at a coarse token count $L_s\!\ll\!L$ per tile. We use
$L\!=\!128,\,L_s\!=\!32,\,d\!=\!128,\,n_l\!=\!3$ in every reported result; the analysis
generalizes verbatim to other choices.

\paragraph{Pipeline and dispatch budget.}
The network is a four-stage pipeline from the simulation state and $A$ to a packed tensor of
factor matrices encoding an $\mathcal{H}$-matrix approximate inverse $M\!\approx\!A^{-1}$:
(1) a \emph{physics-aware encoder} (Sec.~\ref{sec:method:encoder}) lifts per-node and
per-edge features to width-$d$ node embeddings; (2) a \emph{diagonal attention stack}
(Sec.~\ref{sec:method:diag}) processes leaf-grouped tokens and emits one dense factor per
leaf; (3) an \emph{off-diagonal attention stack} (Sec.~\ref{sec:method:offdiag}) processes
tile-grouped tokens and emits one low-rank factor per off-diagonal tile; (4) linear decoder
heads (Sec.~\ref{sec:method:decoder}) produce the final factor tensors. \emph{There are
exactly two attention computations in the entire network} --- one batched over all $K$
leaves and one batched over all $M_\mathcal{H}$ tiles --- so a forward pass dispatches
exactly two attention kernels, independent of $N$. Combined with the static partition and
the absence of data-dependent branching elsewhere, this is what later lets training and PCG
inference run inside a single CUDA Graph.

\subsection{From simulation state to per-node embeddings}
\label{sec:method:encoder}

Because the preconditioner is neural rather than algebraic, the network is not constrained
to ingest only the scalar entries $A_{ij}$: it can directly observe the per-node and
per-pair quantities that the simulator assembles into those entries, recovering information
that a fixed analytical preconditioner would never see. The encoder takes as input whichever
per-node features the simulator already exposes --- typically a subset of position,
velocity, density, pressure, temperature, and material parameters --- together with the
geometric and coupling features $(\Delta\mathbf{x}_{ij}, A_{ij})$ for every nonzero of $A$.
A two-layer MLP lifts the node features (optionally concatenated with a broadcast global
context vector) to dimension $d$, and $n_{\mathrm{gcn}}\!=\!2$ residual
graph-convolutional layers~\cite{kipf2017semi} mix in neighborhood information using $A$ as
the message-passing weight, yielding one width-$d$ embedding per node aware of its local
matrix entries and the physical state of its neighbors. This is the only stage of the
network that observes individual graph edges; everything downstream operates on the block
layout of the partition, decoupling cost from edge count.

\subsection{Diagonal attention stack}
\label{sec:method:diag}

For each of the $K$ leaves, the diagonal stack produces a square factor
$F_k\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{L\times L}$, with the corresponding diagonal block of $M$ given by the
PSD outer product $F_kF_k^\top$. The encoder embeddings inside a leaf are processed at full
per-node resolution by $n_l$ transformer layers~\cite{Liu2021swin,vaswani2017attention} that
restrict attention to within the leaf --- the within-window attention pattern of Swin
adapted to one-dimensional index ranges. Attention logits carry a per-head bias produced by
a small MLP from the four-dimensional edge descriptor
$(\Delta\mathbf{x}_{ij}, A_{ij})$ for each in-leaf token pair, so geometry and coupling
strength can modulate attention directly. This is the first of the two attention dispatches
mentioned above; it is batched over all $K$ leaves.

\subsection{Off-diagonal attention stack}
\label{sec:method:offdiag}

For each off-diagonal tile $m$ spanning row index set $\mathcal{I}_m$ and column index set
$\mathcal{J}_m$ of size $S_mL$, the off-diagonal stack produces a pair of thin factor
matrices $U_m, V_m\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{L_s\times L_s}$ whose outer product
$B_m\!=\!U_mV_m^\top$ is the tile's coarse-resolution representation. Two design choices
shape this stream.

\emph{Constant coarse token count, independent of tile size.} Per-node embeddings are pooled
along the row-strip $\mathcal{I}_m$ and the column-strip $\mathcal{J}_m$ of the encoder
output, then condensed along the in-strip axis to $L_s\!=\!L/p_{\mathrm{off}}$ tokens. Every
tile therefore arrives at the attention stack with the same shape $(L_s, d)$, regardless of
its physical size $S_mL$ --- a $4\times$ in-strip condensation for the smallest tiles
($S_m\!=\!1$) and a $4S_m\times$ condensation for the largest. Attention then runs across the $L_s$
tokens of each tile, batched over all tiles; this is the second (and final) attention
dispatch. Edge biases for each in-tile token pair are computed from the mean of
$(\Delta\mathbf{x}_{ij}, A_{ij})$ over all node pairs spanning the corresponding
row/column sub-strips.

\emph{Constant per-tile rank, hence shrinking rank fraction with separation.} The factor
pair $(U_m, V_m)$ has rank at most $L_s$, while the physical block it represents has size
$S_mL\!\times\!S_mL$; the rank fraction $L_s / (S_mL)$ decreases automatically as $S_m$
grows. This is the structural prior that makes $\mathcal{H}$-matrix compression effective:
distant tiles, encoding smoother long-range couplings, are compressed more aggressively than
near ones, while diagonal blocks remain at full rank (Sec.~\ref{sec:method:diag}). This
distribution of representation capacity --- dense local, low-rank far --- is precisely the
asymptotic-smoothness pattern documented for inverses of discretized elliptic operators
(Sec.~\ref{sec:background}). Fig.~\ref{fig:matrices-and-rank}\,(left) shows that the
trained $M$ visibly reproduces this pattern on real frames: $A$ is the sparse five-point
stencil; the dense inverse $A^{-1}$ exhibits a multiscale block-and-tile pattern with
full-rank diagonal blocks and off-diagonal tiles whose magnitude decays with separation;
the learned $M$ reproduces the same block-and-tile structure --- the network has
recovered the operator-level $\mathcal{H}$-matrix geometry of $A^{-1}$ from per-node
features and the sparse $A$ alone, not a $b$-specific solution.

Fig.~\ref{fig:matrices-and-rank}b is the quantitative core of the structural-bias claim.
For each off-diagonal distance class $S$, we compare the architecture-provided rank
fraction $L_s/(SL)$ against the numerical rank fraction required for $A^{-1}$ tiles at
target relative errors $\varepsilon\!\in\!\{10^{-3},10^{-6},10^{-9}\}$. Two facts matter:
(i) required rank fraction drops as separation increases (far interactions are more
compressible), and (ii) the fixed provided rank remains above required across all classes.
With $L_s\!=\!32$, this yields explicit headroom at strict precision: at
$\varepsilon\!=\!10^{-9}$, provided rank is still $\sim\!2.6\times$ above required in the
largest-distance class. That headroom is exactly what allows one shared off-diagonal rank
budget to cover all farther-interaction blocks without per-tile rank adaptation.

\paragraph{What happens as $N$ grows.}
A natural concern is whether a constant per-tile rank $L_s$ remains adequate as $N$ grows.
Two regimes are distinguished. (i) \emph{Larger simulations at fixed resolution.} If $N$
grows because the simulation domain grows --- more particles, more cells, the same physical
length scales and the same local interaction radius --- the partition simply contains more
tiles of the same maximum size, the singular-value decay rate of each tile is unchanged, and
the per-tile rank budget stays appropriate. This is the regime our experiments target.
(ii) \emph{Finer simulations at fixed domain.} If instead $N$ grows because the
discretization is refined, the largest tile size $S_{\max}L$ grows with it and the numerical
rank required for a fixed tolerance grows logarithmically with $S_{\max}$ for smooth
Green's functions (Sec.~\ref{sec:background}). Because $L$ and $p_{\mathrm{off}}$ are
hyperparameters, the natural response is to target a constant number of leaves $K$ rather
than a constant $L$; this scales $L$ with the domain dimension at the cost of a single
$O(N\log N)$ pooling pass and otherwise leaves the architecture untouched. We do not pursue
this in our experiments since the regime we evaluate does not span a discretization-refinement
octave wide enough to require it.

\subsection{Highway connections}
\label{sec:method:highways}

Within-leaf and within-tile attention build rich local representations but cannot directly
observe the global state. Highway connections restore this by coupling the diagonal and
off-diagonal stacks through three buffers maintained per transformer layer: row and column
\emph{axial} buffers
$\mathbf{r}_{\mathrm{hw}},\mathbf{c}_{\mathrm{hw}}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{B\times N\times d}$,
and a single global summary token
$\mathbf{g}_{\mathrm{hw}}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{B\times d}$ (illustrated in
Fig.~\ref{fig:partition-and-highways}b).

After the attention sublayer of each transformer block, block-token embeddings are
scatter-added into their corresponding rows of the axial buffers (off-diagonal tile tokens
are first repeat-interleaved back to full leaf resolution so each tile contributes uniformly
across its $S$-leaf row and column ranges), and the global token is then updated as the mean
over all active tokens. Before the FFN sublayer, each token concatenates its row, column,
and global highway context with its own embedding and feeds the resulting $4d$-wide vector
to the FFN's input projection. The four channels in each layer --- intra-block attention
(2D, local), row and column highways (1D, axial), and the global token (0D, broadcast) ---
compose a multiscale communication structure that mirrors the band-row-column-global
geometry of the inverse of a banded matrix. The highway adds one $O(Nd)$ scatter-gather per
layer, preserving overall $\Theta(N)$ scaling. Highways are enabled in every result we
report; we ablate the choice of channels in Sec.~\ref{sec:ablation}.

\subsection{Decoder, output, and the geometric picture}
\label{sec:method:decoder}

After both attention stacks, three lightweight decoder heads project token embeddings to
preconditioner factors:
\begin{itemize}
\item A \emph{leaf head} maps each diagonal-stream token (one per node, $L$ per leaf) through
a two-layer MLP and reshapes the result into a per-leaf factor
$F_k\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{L\times L}$. The corresponding diagonal block of $M$ is
$F_kF_k^\top\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{L\times L}$, PSD by construction.
\item Two \emph{off-diagonal heads} map each off-diagonal-stream token (one per coarse
position, $L_s$ per tile) linearly to $L_s$ output channels, yielding factor pairs
$U_m,V_m\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{L_s\times L_s}$.
\item Two \emph{node heads} project the per-node diagonal-stream embeddings linearly to
$L_s$ output channels, producing per-leaf bridge matrices
$\tilde U_k,\tilde V_k\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{L\times L_s}$ that connect the fine node-level
representation to the coarse tile-level representation.
\end{itemize}

\emph{Geometric picture.} The bridge and tile factors together implement an FMM-style
strip-to-strip chain (worked out in detail in Sec.~\ref{sec:application}). Reading along
the off-diagonal contribution to $M\mathbf{r}$ for a single tile: a residual $\mathbf{r}$
restricted to the column-strip $\mathcal{J}_m$ (length $S_mL$) is first contracted along the
strip by the per-leaf bridge $\tilde U_k^\top$ to produce coarse leaf summaries (length
$L_s$ per leaf in $\mathcal{J}_m$); these per-leaf summaries are then summed across the
$S_m$ leaves of the strip into a single tile-level coarse vector of length $L_s$, which
$B_m\!=\!U_mV_m^\top$ ($L_s\!\times\!L_s$) mixes; the result is broadcast back to all
$S_m$ leaves of the row-strip $\mathcal{I}_m$, and finally expanded to full
node-resolution by the per-leaf prolongation $\tilde V_l$. The shape progression is
therefore
\[
  \resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{$
  \underbrace{S_mL}_{\text{strip}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\,\tilde U^\top\,}\;
  \underbrace{S_mL_s}_{\text{thinner strip}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\,\sum_k\,}\;
  \underbrace{L_s}_{\text{tile summary}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\,B_m\,}\;
  \underbrace{L_s}_{\text{tile summary}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\,\text{broadcast}\,}\;
  \underbrace{S_mL_s}_{\text{thinner strip}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\,\tilde V\,}\;
  \underbrace{S_mL}_{\text{strip}}.
  $}
\]
a fine strip that contracts to a coarse strip, aggregates to a single tile summary,
transmits the coarse far-field interaction, and re-expands back to a fine strip. The geometry mirrors the FMM upward-pass / interaction-list /
downward-pass structure.

\emph{Positive-definiteness.}
By construction every diagonal block $F_kF_k^\top$ is PSD, so the block-diagonal
contribution $\bigoplus_k F_kF_k^\top$ is PSD. The full $M$ adds
indefinite low-rank off-diagonal corrections; in our experiments this assembled $M$ has
remained empirically PSD on every test system and PCG has never broken down on the
preconditioned system. Strict SPD enforcement is cheap if it should ever be needed: a
positive diagonal of $F_k$ (via elementwise softplus or exponential, the standard trick used
in prior neural ICs~\cite{Li2023learning,hausner2023neural}), together with a
diagonal-dominant scaling of off-diagonal factors, would guarantee strict positive
definiteness with negligible overhead. We do not enable these in our reported results.

\emph{Jacobi residual.} A learned per-node gate $\lambda_i$ adds a diagonal correction
$\mathrm{diag}(\boldsymbol\lambda)\,\mathrm{diag}(A)^{-1}$ to $M$. Early in training, when the
hierarchical factors approximate $A^{-1}$ poorly, this Jacobi residual provides a cheap
gradient sink that prevents loss stagnation; once the hierarchical factors are useful, the
gate's contribution tends to zero and capacity shifts to the structured branch.

All factors are concatenated into a single packed tensor of width
$P = K L^2 + M_\mathcal{H} L_s^2 + 2NL_s + N$, which is what the apply routine consumes.

\subsection{Complexity}
\label{sec:method:complexity}

Encoder cost is $O(Nd^2)$ for bounded-degree graphs. The diagonal attention stack processes
$K$ leaves of $L$ tokens each through $n_l$ layers: $O(n_l K L^2 d) = O(n_l N L d)$, linear
in $N$ at fixed $L,d,n_l$. The off-diagonal stack processes $M_\mathcal{H}\!=\!O(K)$ tiles of
$L_s$ tokens each: $O(n_l K L_s^2 d) = O(n_l N L_s^2 d / L)$, also linear in $N$. Highway
scatter-gather contributes $O(Nd)$ per layer. The total inference budget is therefore
$\Theta(N)$, with constants determined entirely by the fixed hyperparameters
$L, L_s, d, n_l$.

\section{Preconditioner Application}
\label{sec:application}
% ===========================================================================

PCG calls $M$ on the residual once per iteration; the cost and the GPU behavior of this
apply is what determines the per-frame throughput of any preconditioner in the inner loop.
$M$ is never assembled --- at full resolution its off-diagonal blocks reach
$\tfrac{N}{2}\!\times\!\tfrac{N}{2}$, and materializing them would destroy the factored
low-rank structure that gives the method its $\Theta(N)$ scaling. We keep $M$ in factored
form $\{F_k\}_k \cup \{\tilde U_k,\tilde V_k\}_k \cup \{B_m\}_m$, right-precondition, and
preallocate every intermediate buffer at solve setup from the static partition so the inner
loop never branches and never allocates.

\paragraph{Why every access is contiguous.}
Three properties of the system compound to make every operand in the apply path a stride-1
read over a contiguous tensor. (i) The system $A$ is indexed along a space-filling-curve or
bandwidth-reducing ordering (Sec.~\ref{sec:intro}), so the residual $\mathbf{r}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^N$
lays out spatially-adjacent degrees of freedom in adjacent memory addresses. (ii) The
$\mathcal{H}$-matrix partition is fixed in advance and its leaf and tile boundaries are
aligned with this ordering, so the slice $\mathbf{r}_k = \mathbf{r}[kL:(k\!+\!1)L]$ is a
contiguous view into the residual rather than a gather. (iii) Within each leaf and within
each tile, attention is windowed and produces factor tensors stored as contiguous batched
arrays of shapes $(K,L,L)$, $(M_\mathcal{H},L_s,L_s)$, and $(K,L,L_s)$. These three choices
are not independent: each presupposes the next, and together they reduce the apply to a
fixed sequence of batched GEMMs over preallocated tensors with no gather/scatter
indirection. The CSR SpMV for $A\mathbf{r}$ is the one nominal exception, but the same
ordering also makes the residual's access pattern within $A$ nearly banded, so the SpMV's
gathers hit a small working set rather than the whole vector.

\paragraph{Diagonal contribution.}
A single batched GEMM evaluates $\mathbf{y}^{\mathrm{diag}}_k = F_k\mathbf{r}_k$ across all
$K$ leaves at once. The blocks are dense $L\!\times\!L$, so this stage has high arithmetic
intensity and saturates Tensor Cores. Cost is $O(NL)$ FLOPs.

\paragraph{Off-diagonal contribution: FMM-style four-stage chain.}
The bridge factors $\tilde U_k,\tilde V_k$ project each leaf's residual to a coarse summary;
the tile factors $B_m\!=\!U_m V_m^\top$ couple summaries between well-separated leaf ranges;
the projection is then inverted to deposit the contribution back on full-resolution leaves.
With $\mathbf{r}_k$ the residual on leaf $k$ and $\mathcal{R}_m,\mathcal{C}_m$ the row and
column leaf ranges of tile $m$:
\begin{align}
  \hat{\mathbf{u}}_k &= \tilde U_k^\top\mathbf{r}_k,\qquad
  \hat{\mathbf{v}}_k = \tilde V_k^\top\mathbf{r}_k && \text{(restriction)}\\
  \mathbf{s}^{\mathrm{r}}_m &= \sum_{k\in\mathcal{R}_m}\hat{\mathbf{u}}_k,\qquad
  \mathbf{s}^{\mathrm{c}}_m = \sum_{k\in\mathcal{C}_m}\hat{\mathbf{v}}_k
  && \text{(strip aggr.)}\\
  \mathbf{t}^{\mathrm{c}}_m &= B_m^\top\,\mathbf{s}^{\mathrm{r}}_m,\qquad
  \mathbf{t}^{\mathrm{r}}_m = B_m\,\mathbf{s}^{\mathrm{c}}_m && \text{(coarse coupling)}\\
  \Delta\mathbf{y}_k &= \tilde U_k\,{\textstyle\sum_{m:k\in\mathcal{R}_m}\!\mathbf{t}^{\mathrm{r}}_m}
                    +  \tilde V_k\,{\textstyle\sum_{m:k\in\mathcal{C}_m}\!\mathbf{t}^{\mathrm{c}}_m}
   && \text{(prolong.)}
\end{align}
Stages 1 and 4 are batched GEMMs of shape $(K,L,L_s)$; stage 3 is a batched GEMM of shape
$(M_\mathcal{H},L_s,L_s)$ that fully occupies Tensor Cores; stage 2 is two scatter-adds
whose gather indices are computed once from the static partition and accessed in contiguous
stride at apply time. Total cost is $O(NL_s)$ FLOPs over $K + M_\mathcal{H}$ small matrices.
Symmetry of $M$ means each $B_m$ is reused for both the upper- and lower-triangular
contribution.

\paragraph{Single-graph capture.}
Capture by \verb|torch.cuda.graph| requires the captured region to contain no Python
control flow, no host-side allocation, and no kernels whose launch parameters depend on
runtime data. The apply path described above satisfies all three by construction: the
partition is determined analytically before training and never changes, the workspace is
preallocated, and every kernel's launch shape is fixed by hyperparameters $L,L_s,d,K$. The
complete PCG inner loop --- one CSR SpMV for $A\mathbf{r}$, one preconditioner apply, and
the PCG scalar dot products and SAXPY updates --- therefore records into a single CUDA Graph,
which subsequent iterations replay with one \verb|cudaGraphLaunch|: zero CPU dispatch
overhead, zero kernel-argument serialization, and a fixed end-to-end latency per iteration.

In a real-time engine, the CPU/GPU dispatch latency between successive small kernels is
often what sets the floor on achievable frame time, not raw arithmetic. Methods whose inner
loop \emph{can} be captured as a single graph enjoy a step-function reduction in this floor;
methods whose inner loop \emph{cannot} pay the dispatch overhead on every iteration. We did
not design for graph capture explicitly --- it falls out of the static partition, the
absence of triangular solves, the contiguous data layout, and the fixed launch shapes ---
but the practical consequence is a property we explicitly target with this paper. For
contrast, IC- and DILU-class preconditioners cannot be captured this way because their
triangular solves serialize through data-dependent wavefronts that the runtime cannot
schedule into a single graph, and AMG V-cycles require level-by-level synchronization
barriers that fragment the capture by construction.

\paragraph{Memory access compared.}
Among learned alternatives, neural SPAI~\cite{Yang2025sparse} also avoids triangular solves
and is graph-capturable. Its apply, however, evaluates $\mathbf{y} = G^\top(G\mathbf{r})$ as
two SpMVs against a sparse $G$ inheriting the nonzero pattern of $A$. For each nonzero
$G_{ij}$, the SpMV fetches $\mathbf{r}_j$ from an offset determined entirely by $G$'s
sparsity pattern, not by any block structure; even under a good node ordering the gather
addresses skip across the residual at the irregularity of the underlying graph, leaving the
kernel bandwidth-bound and limited by random-access throughput on the GPU memory
subsystem~\cite{bell2009implementing}. Our apply replaces this irregular-gather access
pattern with batched GEMMs over preallocated contiguous tensors aligned to the partition;
the address arithmetic of every operand is a single stride and the kernel becomes
compute-bound on Tensor Cores. The advantage is not the ordering itself --- which the SPAI
apply could in principle also enjoy --- but the fact that windowed attention coupled to the
$\mathcal{H}$-matrix partition lets us express the apply as dense block work rather than
nonzero-by-nonzero gathers (Table~\ref{tab:apply_complexity}, introduced in
\S\ref{sec:intro}).

\subsection{Cosine Hutchinson Probe Loss}
\label{sec:training:loss}

We train the network self-supervised. Given a batch of probe vectors
$\mathbf{Z}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times K_z}$ drawn from the spectrally-balanced distribution
described below, we compute $A\mathbf{Z}$ via SpMV (treated as a fixed input, with no
gradient through $A$), apply the preconditioner to obtain $MA\mathbf{Z}$, and minimize the
angle between $MA\mathbf{Z}$ and $\mathbf{Z}$ as flattened tensors:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\cos}
  \;=\; 1 \,-\, \frac{\langle \mathbf{Z},\,MA\mathbf{Z}\rangle_F}
        {\|\mathbf{Z}\|_F\,\|MA\mathbf{Z}\|_F},
  \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
where $\langle X,Y\rangle_F = \operatorname{tr}(X^\top Y)$ is the Frobenius inner product and
$\|\!\cdot\!\|_F$ the Frobenius norm. Equivalently, this is the cosine similarity between
$\operatorname{vec}(\mathbf{Z})$ and $\operatorname{vec}(MA\mathbf{Z})$ --- a single global
angle over all $N\!\cdot\!K_z$ entries rather than an average of $K_z$ per-probe cosines.
The single-denominator form ties all probes together through the same normalization, which
we found to give noticeably more stable gradients at small $K_z$ than the per-probe mean.
A perfect preconditioner gives $\mathcal{L}_{\cos}\!=\!0$; the worst case is
$\mathcal{L}_{\cos}\!=\!2$ (anti-aligned).

\paragraph{Why cosine: from vector distance to subspace distance.}
The conceptual upgrade $\mathcal{L}_{\cos}$ makes over Frobenius- and SAI-style objectives
is to replace a distance between \emph{vectors} with a distance between \emph{subspaces}.
Frobenius-type losses --- including the SAI loss
$\bigl\|\tfrac{1}{\|A\|}AM-I\bigr\|_F^2$ used by~\cite{Yang2025sparse} --- penalize the
pointwise Euclidean deviation of the specific vector $\tfrac{1}{\|A\|}AM\mathbf{z}$ from the
specific vector $\mathbf{z}$. That distance is sensitive to the magnitude of $AM\mathbf{z}$,
so it implicitly demands that $AM$ act as identity at a particular absolute scale --- in the
SAI case, at scale $\|A\|$. $\mathcal{L}_{\cos}$, by contrast, sees only the
\emph{direction} of $MA\mathbf{Z}$. Two preconditioners that send a probe to the same
one-dimensional subspace incur the same loss, no matter how they scale the vector inside
that subspace. Geometrically, $\mathcal{L}_{\cos}$ is a distance on the projective space
$\mathbb{P}(\mathbb{R}^{NK_z})$ rather than a Euclidean distance on $\mathbb{R}^{NK_z}$:

\begin{proposition}[Cosine Hutchinson loss is a subspace distance]
\label{prop:cos-subspace}
Let $M:\mathbb{R}^N\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^N$ be a linear preconditioner, $A$ a fixed SPD matrix,
and $\mathbf{Z}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times K_z}$ a probe matrix with
$\mathbf{Z}\!\ne\!0$ and $MA\mathbf{Z}\!\ne\!0$. Write
$\widehat{\mathbf{Z}}\!=\!\operatorname{vec}(\mathbf{Z})$ and
$\widehat{\mathbf{Y}}\!=\!\operatorname{vec}(MA\mathbf{Z})$, both elements of
$\mathbb{R}^{NK_z}$.
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{(Positive-scale invariance.)} For every $\alpha\!>\!0$,
$\mathcal{L}_{\cos}(\alpha M)\!=\!\mathcal{L}_{\cos}(M)$. The loss therefore descends to a
well-defined function on the quotient of preconditioners modulo positive rescaling.
\item \emph{(Subspace interpretation.)} Let $\theta\in[0,\pi/2]$ be the principal angle
between the lines $\mathbb{R}\widehat{\mathbf{Z}}$ and $\mathbb{R}\widehat{\mathbf{Y}}$ in
$\mathbb{R}^{NK_z}$, and let
$\Pi_{\mathbb{R}\mathbf{v}}\!=\!\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^\top\!/\|\mathbf{v}\|_2^2$ denote the
orthogonal projector onto the line through $\mathbf{v}$. Then
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\cos}(M) \;=\; 1-\cos\theta,
  \qquad
  \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl\|\Pi_{\mathbb{R}\widehat{\mathbf{Z}}}
              -\Pi_{\mathbb{R}\widehat{\mathbf{Y}}}\bigr\|_F^2
  \;=\; 1-\cos^2\theta,
  \label{eq:cos-proj}
\end{equation}
so $\mathcal{L}_{\cos}$ depends on $\bigl(\widehat{\mathbf{Z}},\widehat{\mathbf{Y}}\bigr)$
only through the unsigned angle between the lines they span in $\mathbb{R}^{NK_z}$. Both
functionals vanish exactly when those two lines coincide.
\item \emph{(SAI loss is a vector distance.)} The SAI-style loss
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SAI}}(M)\!=\!\bigl\|\,\tfrac{1}{\|A\|}AM\mathbf{Z}-\mathbf{Z}\bigr\|_F^2$
is \emph{not} invariant under $M\!\mapsto\!\alpha M$ for $\alpha\!\ne\!1$, and is minimized
uniquely (over scalings of $M$) at the choice that places the vector
$\tfrac{1}{\|A\|}AM\mathbf{Z}$ as close as possible \emph{as a vector in $\mathbb{R}^{NK_z}$}
to the specific target $\mathbf{Z}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
(1) Scaling $M$ by $\alpha\!>\!0$ scales the numerator
$\langle\mathbf{Z},MA\mathbf{Z}\rangle_F$ by $\alpha$ and the denominator
$\|\mathbf{Z}\|_F\,\|MA\mathbf{Z}\|_F$ by the same $\alpha$, leaving the cosine unchanged.
(2) The first equality is by definition of $\theta$; the second follows from the rank-one
projector identity
$\tfrac{1}{2}\|\Pi_{\mathbb{R}\mathbf{u}}-\Pi_{\mathbb{R}\mathbf{v}}\|_F^2
= 1 - \langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle^2/(\|\mathbf{u}\|^2\|\mathbf{v}\|^2) = 1-\cos^2\theta$.
Both $1\!-\!\cos\theta$ and $1\!-\!\cos^2\theta$ are valid notions of squared chordal
distance on $\mathbb{P}(\mathbb{R}^{NK_z})$ near $\theta\!=\!0$; we use $1-\cos\theta$ in
\eqref{eq:loss} for better-conditioned gradients near the minimum.
(3) $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SAI}}$ has the form $\|\beta M\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{u}\|^2$ for fixed
$\beta\!=\!1/\|A\|$, strictly convex in $\beta M$, hence not rescaling-invariant.
\end{proof}

\noindent
The proposition matches the PCG geometry one-to-one. PCG's convergence depends only on the
relative spread of the eigenvalues of $MA$, not on their absolute location
(Sec.~\ref{sec:background}); accordingly, the correct space to optimize $M$ over is the
projective space $M/\mathbb{R}_{>0}$, and the natural loss on that space is a distance
between the subspaces $\mathbb{R}\widehat{\mathbf{Z}}$ and $\mathbb{R}\widehat{\mathbf{Y}}$
they span, exactly what $\mathcal{L}_{\cos}$ provides. Frobenius- and SAI-style losses live
on the wrong space --- they pin down the absolute scale of $MA$ even though PCG does not
care --- and as a side effect implicitly demand that the eigenvalues of $MA$ cluster near a
chosen value ($\|A\|$ in the SAI case), wasting capacity on a constraint with no algorithmic
payoff. By dropping that constraint, $\mathcal{L}_{\cos}$ frees the network to cluster the
spectrum wherever the current preconditioner makes angular alignment easiest --- a behavior
we observe directly in \S\ref{sec:results:training}
(Figs.~\ref{fig:training:dynamics}--\ref{fig:loss:eigenvalues}) and ablate against SAI in
\S\ref{sec:ablation}.

\subsection{Spectrally Biased Probe Vectors}

An isotropic Gaussian probe places equal expected energy on every eigenmode of $MA$
(Sec.~\ref{sec:background}), so the resulting gradient signal is also white in the probe's
eigenbasis. The blocks of our preconditioner, however, are not all at the same spatial
scale: leaf-diagonal blocks resolve fine, high-frequency structure over $L$ adjacent nodes,
while an off-diagonal tile of size $SL$ resolves much lower-frequency, larger-scale
structure. A spectrally white probe distribution therefore distributes the gradient signal
unevenly across these block scales --- the high-frequency components, which the
fine-scale (diagonal) blocks are tuned to, dominate the signal; the coarse-scale
(off-diagonal, large-$S$) blocks receive proportionally weaker gradients. The downstream
effect is that blocks at different scales saturate at different times during training, with
the coarse blocks plateauing late and limiting overall convergence.

We rebalance the gradient signal across block scales by shifting probe energy toward lower
spatial frequencies. A small number of damped-Jacobi smoothing steps acts as a spectral
low-pass on the probe:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}^{(t+1)} = \mathbf{z}^{(t)} \,-\, \omega\,D^{-1} A\,\mathbf{z}^{(t)},\qquad
  D = \operatorname{diag}(A),
  \label{eq:jacobi_smooth}
\end{equation}
with $\omega\!=\!0.6$ and two steps in every reported run. The high-frequency components of
the probe are damped more strongly than the low-frequency components, redistributing probe
energy toward the eigenmodes the coarse blocks are responsible for. The result is more
even gradient magnitudes across block scales and substantially more synchronous training of
fine and coarse blocks. Probes are detached, so gradients do not flow back through the
smoothing.

\subsection{Training Setup}

We train with AdamW under a reduce-on-plateau schedule and global gradient clipping. Training
contexts (graph, $A$, masks, padded sizes, smoothed probes) are precomputed once and cached
on disk; at each step a mini-batch is drawn at random and padded to a common node count. We
set the number of probe vectors to $K_z = \max(64,\lceil\sqrt{N}\rceil)$, balancing gradient
noise against compute. The model is compiled with \verb|torch.compile|. Preconditioner
weights and the apply path use float32; only the PCG scalar accumulators (dot products,
residual norms, step sizes) are computed in float64, which we found is sufficient to
prevent residual drift on stiff systems without paying for full mixed-precision GEMMs.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:results:setup}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

All GPU experiments run on one NVIDIA H200 per job. Our model uses $d\!=\!128$,
$n_l\!=\!3$, $L\!=\!128$, $p_{\mathrm{diag}}\!=\!1$, $p_{\mathrm{off}}\!=\!4$,
$n_{\mathrm{gcn}}\!=\!2$, highways enabled, trained once for $\sim\!15$\,min and reused
at every $N$. All reported solve times are to relative residual
$\|\mathbf{r}_k\|_2 / \|\mathbf{r}_0\|_2 \!\le\! 10^{-8}$ --- two to three orders of
magnitude tighter than the $\sim\!10^{-3}$ tolerance typical of graphics-grade pressure
projection, chosen so comparisons reflect preconditioner quality rather than early
termination; at looser tolerances every method finishes in fewer iterations but the
relative ordering is preserved. GPU timings are taken after one warmup pass; PCG timing
uses single-graph CUDA Graph replay (\S\ref{sec:application}) for every method that
admits it (unpreconditioned CG, Jacobi, AMGX SPAI, ours), and per-kernel launches for
IC- and AMG-class methods that do not. AMGX baselines run with vendor defaults; we
verified at $N\!=\!8\,192$ that no nearby AMGX hyperparameter configuration meaningfully
improves total time. Full hardware, software stack, precision, and baseline-tuning
details are in Supplementary Material, \S\ref{SX-supp:experimental-setup}.
We additionally include neural SPAI~\cite{Yang2025sparse} as a learned
baseline: we ported its training pipeline and SAI loss to our multiphase
benchmark and re-trained one network per scale, applying the resulting
$G$ as a CUDA-Graph-captured pair of SpMVs in PCG.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Target regime}
\label{sec:results:regime}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Many interactive physics-simulation pipelines --- graphics-grade fluid simulators, cloth
and contact dynamics, soft-body and deformable solvers used in games and visual effects,
real-time finite-element solvers for haptics and surgical simulation, control-stack solvers
for robotics --- spend a substantial fraction of their per-frame budget on a sparse SPD
solve in which $A$ is rebuilt every time step (representative scenes in
Fig.~\ref{fig:teaser}). The bottleneck in these settings is rarely raw size; the systems
involved typically resolve to $N \!\sim\! 10^3$--$10^5$ unknowns per frame. The bottleneck
is conditioning and connectivity: heterogeneous material contrast (multiphase fluids,
stiff-soft contact, anisotropic media) drives the condition number up, and complex domain
topology (closed barriers, near-disconnected sub-regions, internal boundaries) forces
long-range coupling that local preconditioners cannot resolve in a few iterations.

The regime targeted in this paper is: \emph{stiff,
every-frame-different SPD systems with a hard real-time budget}.

\paragraph{Why this regime is hard.} Two structural properties of the SPD systems in this
class break the standard preconditioner families. First, the coupling is fundamentally
\emph{non-local}: closed barriers, anisotropic media, and stiff-soft contact create
near-disconnected sub-regions whose pressures or stresses equilibrate only through long
chains of interactions across the domain. Jacobi and IC-class preconditioners damp the
high-frequency component of the residual but leave the long-wavelength component intact;
their iteration count scales with the contrast ratio. AMG can resolve the long range, but
its V-cycle synchronization and irregular memory pattern cost more per iteration than the
inner loop has to spend. Second, the operator \emph{geometry does not line up with
preconditioner geometry}: the stencil of $A$ is local, but the support of $A^{-1}$ is
global and multiscale, and that mismatch is exactly what an $\mathcal{H}$-matrix structural
prior is designed to capture. Our benchmark (Sec.~\ref{sec:results:benchmark}) emphasizes
challenging instances of this regime: per-frame randomization of barrier topology and
contrast increases the incidence of near-disconnected, high-contrast configurations.

We explicitly do not target massive regular voxel grids ($N\!\gg\!10^{6}$), where
structured multigrid amortizes its setup across many similar solves and
dominates~\cite{Lyu2026multigrid}, nor batched offline PDE workloads where setup cost is
irrelevant.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Benchmark}
\label{sec:results:benchmark}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

We instantiate the regime above as a multiphase pressure-Poisson problem: the sparse SPD
system that arises in the pressure-projection step of an incompressible fluid simulator,
with a heterogeneous density field driving multi-scale coupling and elevating the condition
number. We choose pressure-Poisson for ubiquity --- it is one of the most common SPD systems
that appears in graphics-grade specialized simulators across fluid, MPM, and fractional-step
pipelines --- and pick the 2D structured-grid instance as our quantitative benchmark.
Although our architecture handles 3D directly --- demonstrated in the teaser
(Fig.~\ref{fig:teaser}) --- we deliberately evaluate in 2D because it is a
\emph{harder} setting for local preconditioners: in 2D each node has only $4$ neighbors,
so an IC factor or SPAI row carries strictly less local coupling information than in 3D,
while the stiffness driven by density contrast is unchanged. Sparser connectivity means
each iteration of a local preconditioner closes less of the residual gap, so convergence
is slower and the advantage of a long-range preconditioner is more pronounced. On a
structured grid the classical Cartesian-grid baselines (IC, AMG) are also at their
strongest, so any gap we report is against well-tuned competition. We further restrict
to structured grids for the quantitative study because structured pressure-Poisson is the
dominant bottleneck in target graphics simulation pipelines. Architecturally, the method
does not assume regular grid connectivity; it requires only a sparse neighborhood graph and
a loose spatial ordering of degrees of freedom. We leave direct unstructured-mesh
validation to future work. Comparable learned-preconditioner work in graphics
evaluates on problems of essentially the same order of
magnitude~\cite{Li2023learning,hausner2023neural,Yang2025sparse}. Our preconditioner is
trained once offline (approximately $15$\,min on a single H200) and then applied with zero
in-the-loop setup cost; this is exactly the regime where classical methods that cannot reuse
factorizations must repeatedly pay setup.
Fig.~\ref{fig:bench-frame} shows a representative frame.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{multiphase_hero.png}
  \caption{A representative frame from our multiphase pressure-Poisson benchmark at
    $N\!=\!16\,384$. \emph{Left:} heterogeneous density field $\rho$ with $27\!\times$
    contrast, separated by closed and partially-closed barriers (yellow). \emph{Middle:}
    buoyancy-driven right-hand side $b\!=\!-\partial_y \rho$, concentrated on horizontal
    density interfaces. \emph{Right:} recovered pressure $p$ with velocity
    $-\nabla p / \rho$ overlaid.}
  \label{fig:bench-frame}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Discretization.}
Each frame is a 2D structured grid with $N$ cells, indexed along a Morton (Z-curve) curve.
The assembled $A$ is the standard 5-point pressure-Poisson Laplacian
$A_{ii}\!=\!\sum_j w_{ij}$, $A_{ij}\!=\!-w_{ij}$ for neighbors, with face conductances
$w_{ij}\!=\!2\rho_i\rho_j/(\rho_i\!+\!\rho_j)$ (harmonic mean) on a per-cell density field
$\rho:\Omega\!\to\!\mathbb{R}_{>0}$. The harmonic-mean discretization is the standard
finite-volume choice for pressure projection on a piecewise-constant density field, and is
the workhorse for pressure-projection steps in graphics-grade specialized fluid simulators
(FLIP/PIC, MPM, fractional-step Navier--Stokes), modulo boundary-condition specifics.

\paragraph{Per-frame randomization.}
Each frame independently randomizes three axes designed to stress the preconditioner across
the multi-scale, multi-topology regime described above (full generation procedure in
Supplementary Material, \S\ref{SX-supp:dataset}):
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Multiphase density contrast.} $\rho_{\text{light}}\!=\!1$ outside barriers;
inside barriers, $\rho_{\text{heavy}}$ is drawn log-uniformly from $[5,100]$ each frame, so
condition numbers span roughly $5\!\times$--$100\!\times$ the pure-Laplacian baseline within
a single dataset.
\item \emph{Topology.} The barrier region itself is the union of $1$--$3$ rectangular
barriers, each independently vertical or horizontal (random center and thickness), with the
gap topology drawn from \{top, bottom, middle hole, \emph{closed}\}. Closed barriers create
near-disconnected sub-domains where pressure equilibrates almost entirely through long-range
coupling --- the regime where local preconditioners lose conditioning.
\item \emph{Orientation.} Both vertical and horizontal barriers appear, exercising the
row-strip and column-strip highway buffers symmetrically (Sec.~\ref{sec:method:highways}).
\end{itemize}
A small additive noise ($\sigma\!=\!5\%$) is applied to $\rho$ pointwise so no two frames
share an identical density field. The dataset is small enough to release in full
($\sim\!120$\,frames per scale at the largest scale, $\sim\!200$\,MB total binary).
Across this distribution, per-frame condition numbers span roughly
$\kappa(A)\!\in\![10^{3},10^{5}]$. This benchmark therefore isolates the exact deployment
condition we target: high-contrast, topology-varying SPD systems with per-frame matrix
changes, where setup-heavy preconditioners lose their usual amortization advantage.

\paragraph{Setup-amortization savings (frame-equivalent).}
At $N\!=\!8\,192$, Table~\ref{tab:full_perf_table_main} reports $17.9$\,ms for ours versus
$134.5$\,ms for AMGX SPAI and $503.0$\,ms for IC / DILU, i.e.\ per-frame gaps of
$116.6$\,ms and $485.1$\,ms in the changing-$A$ regime where those baselines cannot reuse
setup. Using our one-time offline training cost ($\sim\!15$\,min), the crossover is
approximately $7.7\!\times\!10^3$ frames versus AMGX SPAI and
$1.9\!\times\!10^3$ frames versus IC / DILU.

\paragraph{Why the measured curve is not perfectly linear.}
Theoretical complexity is linear in $N$ for our apply and inference path
(\S\ref{sec:method:complexity}), but measured wall-clock time on GPUs is
piecewise nonlinear. As $N$ grows, kernels cross hardware thresholds:
warp- and tile-quantization effects (fixed thread-block and Tensor-Core MMA
shapes), cache working-set transitions (L1/L2 reuse changing once tensors no
longer fit), and occupancy shifts from register/shared-memory pressure. These
architecture effects change constants with scale even when the algorithmic
order remains $\Theta(N)$.

\paragraph{Main result.}
Fig.~\ref{fig:scale_methods} reports per-frame mean$\pm$std solve time across $20$ test
frames per scale; Tables~\ref{tab:full_perf_table_main} and~\ref{tab:neural_time_components_main}
consolidate the same data with
per-method iteration counts. Our method runs at interactive framerates across the full
range: $17.9$\,ms ($\sim\!56$\,fps, $168$ iters) at $N\!=\!8\,192$ and $47.6$\,ms
($\sim\!21$\,fps, $394$ iters) at $N\!=\!16\,384$. The closest GPU baseline that
delivers convergence in the same regime is Jacobi, at $39.5$\,ms ($968$ iters) and
$65.7$\,ms ($1\,543$ iters) respectively --- a $\sim\!4\!\times$--$6\!\times$ smaller
iteration count on identical hardware and a $1.4$--$2.2\!\times$ wall-clock gap.
The learned neural SPAI baseline of Yang \emph{et al.}~\cite{Yang2025sparse},
re-trained per scale on our benchmark and running its CUDA apply path, lands at
$48.1$\,ms ($338$ iters) and $70.9$\,ms ($496$ iters), trailing our method by
$2.7\times$ at $N\!=\!8\,192$ and $1.5\times$ at $N\!=\!16\,384$ despite an
iteration count within $\sim\!2\!\times$ of ours --- the gap is dominated by the
two random-gather SpMVs that its apply must dispatch per iteration, in contrast to our
single batched-GEMM apply on contiguous tensors (\S\ref{sec:application}).
The IC- and AMG-class baselines achieve very low iteration counts ($\sim\!30$--$40$
for GPU \textsc{multicolor\_dilu}, $1$--$3$ for AMGX SPAI), but their per-iteration
cost is dominated by sequential triangular solves or level-by-level V-cycle
synchronization, putting GPU AMG at $\sim\!5$\,fps and GPU \textsc{multicolor\_dilu}
below $2$\,fps at $N\!=\!16\,384$ --- confirming the architectural argument from
\S\ref{sec:application} (Table~\ref{tab:apply_complexity}).

Fig.~\ref{fig:convergence} (figures-only page) traces how convergence actually looks
on a challenging frame --- a closed cross-shaped barrier with stiff density
contrast chosen to maximize long-range coupling --- across five preconditioner families:
unpreconditioned CG, Jacobi, IC, AMG, and ours. The right-hand-side $b$ is supported on
the thin horizontal density interfaces; a useful preconditioner has to propagate that
signal across the whole domain. Fig.~\ref{fig:convergence} directly visualizes this
transport claim: the top row provides the physical context ($\rho$, $b$, pressure,
velocity, and learned $M$ structure), and the residual rows show how each method
moves or fails to move error beyond local stencil neighborhoods. By $k\!=\!1$, Jacobi and IC have only damped the
residual locally around $b$'s support, leaving long-wavelength error along and across
the barrier untouched. AMG and ours both attenuate the residual across the entire
domain after a single application --- the difference is that AMG pays for it with
V-cycle synchronization on every step, while ours dispatches a sequence of batched
GEMMs and captures the inner loop in a single CUDA Graph (\S\ref{sec:application}).
This is the global information-routing pattern the highway buffers implement in our
architecture (\S\ref{sec:method:highways}): impulse-like residuals reach the far side of
the domain after one apply --- i.e., information is routed to locations far beyond each
cell's nearest-neighbor stencil in the earliest iterations --- similar in spread to AMG
and much faster than Jacobi or IC.
The per-method iteration counts to $\mathrm{rtol}\!=\!10^{-8}$ on this frame ($288$,
$1097$, $334$, $19$, $384$ for unpreconditioned CG, Jacobi, IC, AMG, and ours) span
nearly two orders of magnitude in iteration count, but the per-iteration cost ordering
is reversed for AMG and IC (\S\ref{sec:application}): AMG converges in $19$ iterations
yet runs slowest in wall time because of V-cycle synchronization, while Jacobi's
diagonal scaling is counter-productive on this stiff multiphase frame and inflates the
iteration count above unpreconditioned CG.

The scaling behavior of the gap is itself informative. At small $N$, where the blocks of
$A$ are well-conditioned and bandwidth dominates, the preconditioner-quality advantage
matters less and our gain over Jacobi is $1.8\times$. The advantage peaks at $3.55\times$
at $N\!=\!4\,096$, where contrast and stiffness bite but Jacobi's iteration count has
already begun to blow up. By $N\!=\!16\,384$, our $1.36\times$ absolute advantage comes
from a $\sim\!4\times$ smaller iteration count ($386$ vs.\ $1\,480$) rather than from
per-iteration speed. Standard deviations across our 20 test frames track condition
number rather than $N$: unpreconditioned CG and Jacobi both show $\sigma/\mu\!\approx\!50$--$100\%$
at $N\!\ge\!8\,192$ because individual frames span the full $[5,100]$ contrast range,
while our $\sigma/\mu$ stays at $\le\!21\%$ even at $N\!=\!16\,384$ --- the
data-driven prior is robust to the per-frame variation that breaks fixed-pattern
preconditioners.

loss on the same checkpoints, and the resulting PCG iteration count across training. The
three curves move together up to step $\sim\!8\,000$, after which the SAI loss
\emph{rises} from $\sim\!10^{-3}$ to $\sim\!0.5$ while $\mathcal{L}_{\cos}$ continues to
decrease and PCG iteration count continues to fall in lockstep with it. The model is
moving the eigenvalues of $MA$ \emph{away} from $\lambda\!=\!\|A\|$ in pointwise terms
while moving them \emph{toward} a tighter relative cluster wherever the residual makes
alignment easiest --- the behavior predicted in Sec.~\ref{sec:training:loss}, and direct
evidence for dropping the eigenvalue-location prior.

Fig.~\ref{fig:loss:eigenvalues} confirms this directly on the spectrum at $N\!=\!1\,024$
(the largest scale where a full eigendecomposition is tractable). An SAI-trained model
delivers a $16\times$ condition-number reduction with the cluster anchored near
$\lambda\!=\!\|A\|$; a cosine-trained model with the identical architecture delivers a
$68\times$ reduction with the cluster settled near $\lambda\!\approx\!0.08$ --- the gap is
attributable to the loss alone. Total wall-clock training is $16.2$\,min on a single H200
($24\,300$ steps); the model overtakes GPU Jacobi at step $\sim\!2\,000$ ($\sim\!1.3$\,min)
and drops under $200$ PCG iterations by step $\sim\!12\,000$ ($\sim\!8$\,min).
Fig.~\ref{fig:probe-alignment} complements this view by showing probe-space alignment
statistics at $N\!=\!8\,192$ over training. It ties the loss trajectory directly to
spectral clustering behavior and downstream PCG improvements.

\subsection{Ablations}
\label{sec:ablation}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

We ablate width $d$, depth $n_l$, and highway connections. The loss ablation is the
training-dynamics comparison in \S\ref{sec:results:training}. The default configuration
($d\!=\!128$, $n_l\!=\!3$, highways on) has $\sim\!2.6$\,M trainable parameters.
Sweep variants in Table~\ref{tab:ablation} span $\sim\!0.7$\,M ($d\!=\!64$) to
$\sim\!10.5$\,M ($d\!=\!256$), all small by neural-operator standards. The three groups
report results at different scale sets. Within each group, every variant uses the same
scale, the same 20-frame held-out test set, and the same convergence criterion.

Three observations from Table~\ref{tab:ablation}: (i) \emph{width}~--- $d\!=\!128$ is the
most reliable choice across scales. $d\!=\!256$ is competitive at $N\!\le\!4\,096$, but
its iteration count nearly triples at $N\!=\!8\,192$ (likely an optimization issue on the
sparser training signal per parameter at larger $N$). $d\!=\!64$ is $28\%$ slower overall.
(ii) \emph{depth}~--- the effect is large and monotone. Reducing $n_l\!=\!3\!\to\!1$ cuts
inference $2.6\!\times$ but doubles total solve time because PCG iterations rise
$2.5\!\times$. Additional layers are needed to compose information routed through the
highway tokens. (iii) \emph{highways}~--- removing them saves $20\%$ training time, but
raises PCG iterations $2.3\!\times$ at $N\!=\!2\,048$. The penalty grows with $N$: without
highways, each block optimizes a purely local factor with no awareness of the bulk
spectral shift.

\subsection{Generalization}
\label{sec:results:generalization}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

Our generalization scope is \emph{within-family deployment robustness}: resilience to
configuration drift within the same multiphase Poisson simulator family, not cross-family
transfer. We test the three deployment-relevant OOD shifts: topology/layout (closed barriers
withheld from training), parameter-range (low-contrast train, higher-contrast eval), and
compositional OOD (both shifts together). Table~\ref{tab:generalization} reports the four
most informative (train, eval) cells at $N\!=\!4\,096$; additional robustness statistics
(non-convergence, p95/p99, OOD/ID degradation) are included in the supplementary outputs.

\paragraph{Topology generalization.}
A model trained entirely without closed-barrier frames matches---and marginally
exceeds---in-distribution iteration counts when evaluated on a closed-barrier-only
test set ($68$ vs.\ $82$ iters), preserving the $3.3\times$ total-time speedup over
Jacobi. The $\mathcal{H}$-matrix partition is defined purely by the spatial indexing
of degrees of freedom and encodes no barrier geometry, so the network adapts without
retraining.

\paragraph{Contrast generalization.}
Training on low-contrast frames ($\rho_\text{heavy}\!\in\![5,25]$) and evaluating on
high-contrast frames ($\rho_\text{heavy}\!\in\!(25,100]$) roughly doubles iteration
count ($142$ vs.\ $82$) and raises total solve time from $8.8$ to $13.1$\,ms, while
preserving a $1.9\times$ speedup over Jacobi. Variance grows substantially
($\pm\!10$\,ms vs.\ $\pm\!2$\,ms in-distribution), reflecting that a subset of
high-contrast frames fall outside the learned eigenvalue cluster. The mechanism is
structural. Note that pure amplitude growth is handled for free by the scale-invariance
of $\mathcal{L}_{\cos}$: what escapes the learned cluster is not a global rescaling.
At high contrast, far-field interactions across sharp density interfaces carry
substantially more relative weight than in any training frame; the network generalizes
up to the regime it has seen, but the degree to which long-range coupling must dominate
at the highest contrasts lies beyond that boundary.
In the compositional
worst-case cell (\emph{closed barriers + high contrast}), speedup remains $1.9\times$.
Together these results indicate that topology generalization is inexpensive and the main
OOD cost is contrast/stiffness shift.

\section{Discussion and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ===========================================================================

The recipe of this paper --- a weak-admissibility $\mathcal{H}$-matrix prior, a
scale-invariant cosine-Hutchinson objective, and a single-graph apply path --- is most
useful in the regime it targets: stiff, every-frame-different SPD systems
with a hard real-time budget. Within that regime, on the multiphase pressure-Poisson
family we evaluate, our method delivers convergence-quality solves at interactive
framerates across the full size range while every baseline we compare against falls
below that bar at the largest $N$
($154$\,fps at $N\!=\!1\,024$ down to $22$\,fps at $N\!=\!16\,384$, versus
$85$\,fps down to $16$\,fps for the closest GPU baseline). The remaining work follows
the contours of that regime in three directions.

\paragraph{Generalization across the regime.} In this paper we evaluate the
generalization claim at the deployment boundary we target: within-family configuration
drift in multiphase Poisson (\S\ref{sec:results:generalization}). The architecture
depends on nothing fluid-specific --- only a loose spatial ordering of degrees of
freedom --- and extends directly to 3D (Fig.~\ref{fig:teaser}). The same design is
compatible with unstructured meshes because regular connectivity is not required (it would
only tighten the bound on GCN passes), but we do not provide unstructured-mesh experiments
in this paper. We evaluate on structured grids because they are the representative bottleneck
in the target pipelines, not as a limitation. Transfer to non-fluid SPD families ---
cloth, contact stiffness, and other interactive physics --- is the next empirical
frontier. Our prediction is that the largest gains will persist on SPD families with
(i) geometric locality, (ii) frame-to-frame coefficient changes, and (iii) hard
real-time budgets, because those are exactly the conditions under which setup-heavy
preconditioners cannot amortize. Concretely, we expect strong transfer to variable-coefficient
elliptic solves in interactive pipelines (multiphase pressure, implicit viscosity/diffusion,
and related implicit-projection substeps), and comparatively smaller gains in regimes where a
single matrix is reused long enough for heavy classical setup to amortize. We are running a
head-to-head against the sparse-approximate-inverse GNN of Yang
\emph{et al.}~\cite{Yang2025sparse} on a shared benchmark as a first direct test of this prediction.

\paragraph{Problem-size flexibility.} Training pre-sizes the partition and apply-path
workspace to a maximum $N$. Inference at smaller $N$ remains time-efficient
(compute scales with active leaves) but not memory-efficient (the GPU footprint stays
at the training maximum), and pushing past the trained maximum requires retraining.
The dynamic-partition variant discussed in \S\ref{sec:method:offdiag} --- constant
leaf count at $O(N\log N)$ rather than constant leaf size at $O(N)$ --- removes this
ceiling at the cost of one extra pooling pass per layer, and is a concrete
near-term architectural direction.

\paragraph{Where the structural prior breaks.} The $\mathcal{H}$-matrix prior assumes
some spatial locality of $A$ under its indexing. The prior degrades where that
geometry is absent: operators with no natural spatial coordinate (power-grid
Laplacians, electrical-circuit and social-network matrices, dense kernel matrices
without spatial embedding) and integral operators whose far-field rank does not decay
with separation. The architecture is not specific to structured grids and is compatible
with unstructured meshes that admit a loose spatial ordering, but this paper does not
empirically validate that setting. The
underlying prior is geometric, and problems without that geometry are out of scope.
This scope statement also gives a falsifiable prediction: when geometric ordering is
available, the recipe should retain its advantage; when it is not, performance should
approach conventional sparse-algebra baselines.
